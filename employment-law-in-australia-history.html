Employment Law In Australia History

Introduction

Australia, like any other country, has a deep-rooted history when it comes to employment law. Since the country's colonization in 1788, there have been various changes to the employment laws, making it an interesting topic to explore. In this article, we will take a closer look at the history of employment law in Australia, its evolution, and its current state.

Early Employment Laws

In the early years of colonization, there were no formal employment laws in place. The colonial powers relied on the common law system that was in place in England at the time. As a result, workers were not protected, and there were no regulations to govern the relationship between employers and employees.

Industrial Revolution and the Rise of Unions

The industrial revolution in the 19th century brought about significant changes to the Australian economy. With the growth of industries, the number of workers increased, and so did the need for regulation. In response to this, unions emerged, fighting for better working conditions and higher wages.

The Harvester Judgment

In 1907, the famous Harvester Judgment was passed, which established the concept of a "fair and reasonable" wage. This judgment set a minimum wage for workers and gave them the right to collective bargaining.

The 20th Century

The 20th century saw a significant shift in employment law in Australia. In 1904, the Commonwealth Conciliation and Arbitration Act was passed, which established a system of industrial relations. This system was based on conciliation and arbitration, which aimed to resolve disputes between employers and employees.

The Fair Work Act

In 2009, the Fair Work Act was introduced, which replaced the previous Workplace Relations Act. This act aimed to provide a fair and balanced system for both employers and employees. It established the Fair Work Commission, which is responsible for regulating the employment relationship and resolving disputes.

Current State of Employment Law

Currently, Australia has one of the most comprehensive employment law systems in the world. The Fair Work Act provides a framework for minimum employment standards, including minimum wages, leave entitlements, and termination provisions. The system also provides for the protection of employees from unfair dismissal and discrimination.

Conclusion

In conclusion, employment law in Australia has come a long way since the early years of colonization. The evolution of employment law has been driven by the growth of industries, the rise of unions, and the need for regulation. The current system provides a comprehensive framework for the employment relationship and is designed to protect the interests of both employers and employees.
